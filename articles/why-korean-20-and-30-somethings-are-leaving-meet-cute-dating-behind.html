A Change in Tone

Romance has long depended on a flattering fiction: that the right person appears at the right time, and the relationship unfolds almost by accident. In Korean dating slang, 자만추 refers to that ideal of meeting someone “naturally,” without an app, an arranged introduction, or overt strategy. The source title asks why people in their twenties and thirties no longer pursue that model in the same way.

That question lands because it is not really about slang. It is about temperament. A generation once taught to admire spontaneity now seems more willing to admit that connection often requires design: clearer intentions, clearer contexts, and a little less reliance on fate.

The End of Passive Romance

There is something elegant about the old fantasy of effortless connection, but it depends on conditions that many adults no longer trust. Natural encounters require time, stable routines, overlapping social worlds, and the confidence that ambiguity will eventually resolve itself. Those conditions are less reliable than they once seemed.

For people in their twenties and thirties, romance now competes with work schedules, fragmented cities, digital habits, and a general exhaustion with mixed signals. Under those pressures, “natural” can start to mean vague, slow, or emotionally expensive. Intentional dating may feel less poetic, but it can also feel more adult. It asks fewer people to pretend they are not looking for what they are clearly looking for.

Why It Matters

This is why the source belongs in THE INTIMACY. Good modern dating is not only about chemistry; it is also about manners. There is a quiet sophistication in choosing clarity over theatrical chance. To say what you want, to enter the right setting, to allow mutual interest to be visible rather than guessed at: these are not cynical habits. They are disciplined ones.

The more interesting shift is not that romance has become less sincere. It may be becoming less performative. Instead of treating seriousness as embarrassing and ambiguity as desirable, many younger adults appear to be revaluing directness. That does not kill mystery. It simply relocates it. The mystery is no longer whether two people are interested, but whether they can build a form of attention that lasts.

A More Honest Courtship

The cultural appeal of the meet-cute will survive. It is too neat, too cinematic, and too flattering to disappear. But as a practical standard for adult connection, it may no longer feel persuasive. The newer preference is not unromantic. It is simply less naive about logistics, timing, and emotional labor.

If the source’s title captures a real mood, then the story is not that younger adults have given up on intimacy. It is that they are editing out some of its decorative myths. In their place comes a quieter proposition: romance can still be elegant, even when it begins on purpose.
